Plate I — The Living Map

Boston, in motion

Drawn from the network’s own geometry, not a road map. Each mark is a real train, gliding as the feed updates. Rest on a station for its arrivals and its record; rest on a line to follow its trains. Or scrub time backward and replay the day.

Plate III — The Record

When they say ten minutes, how long do you wait?

The observatory’s reason for existing. Promises are sampled at standard horizons, arrivals are inferred with documented rules and published uncertainty, and the ledger below cannot be edited by anyone — including the agencies it grades.

Plate IV — The Ledger, Lately

Promises, as objects

Each card is one real promise from the record: when it was issued, what it swore, and what actually happened. Reconstructed exactly from the ledger — these are not examples, they are entries.

Plate V — The Almanac

The days, kept

Each square is one day of each agency’s record — greener days kept their promises, brick days broke them. Click any day to open its diary entry. The calendar fills for as long as the observatory runs.

Colophon — What This Edition Awaits

  • Replay Boston. The observatory has recorded train positions since July 13, 2026; a proper replay viewer returns once the archive holds storms and rush hours worth scrubbing.
  • Station encyclopedia. Reliability score, wait-time history, and peak-hour portraits per station — unlocked as each station accrues graded arrivals.
  • Delay forecasting. Probability a given promise holds, with honest confidence intervals — needs weeks of record before it would be honest to publish.
  • Caltrain. Supported in the pipeline behind a free 511.org key.
